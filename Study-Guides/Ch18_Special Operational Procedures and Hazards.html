✓

✓Noise abatement departure NOT below 240 m (800 ft) AAE

ADF · AAF · Cold soaking · Performance effects · 0.40 mm
De-Icing vs Anti-Icing
DE-ICING
Removes existing contamination
Uses: ADF (Aircraft De-Icing Fluid)
Removes: Frost · Snow · Ice · Slush
ANTI-ICING
Prevents future contamination
Uses: AAF (Aircraft Anti-Icing Fluid)
Applied on clean surface only
De/Anti-icing fluids only needed until aircraft becomes airborne. After takeoff, onboard anti-icing systems take over.

Cold Soaking & Clear Ice
Cold Soaking: Ice can form even when OAT > 0°C because wing skin may be below freezing from cold fuel in tanks.
→Long cruise at high altitude
→Cold fuel in wing tanks
→Rapid descent into humid air

Wet vs Dry Microburst
🌧 Wet Microburst
Associated with heavy rain and humid air. Visible rain shaft.
🏜 Dry Microburst — VIRGA
Rain evaporates before reaching ground. Called VIRGA. No visible rain at surface — very dangerous.
Recovery: Full power + pitch attitude near maximum AOA limits. Best defense = avoid completely. Once inside, expect multiple microbursts nearby.

⚠️ DGCA Exam Traps — Chapter 18
🪤
MMEL vs MEL — who prepares?
MMEL = prepared by Manufacturer, approved by DGCA. MEL = prepared by Operator, approved by DGCA. Very common mix-up.
🪤
Class D fire — Never Halon
Metal fires (Class D) require Dry Powder ONLY. Halon is NEVER used on Class D. Using wrong agent can worsen the fire.
🪤
Explosive vs Rapid decompression
Explosive = <0.5 seconds. Rapid = faster than lungs can decompress (may also cause lung damage). Both require same crew actions.
🪤
Mask deployment altitudes
Warning = 10,000 ft. Masks = 13,200–14,000 ft. Maximum = 15,000 ft. Emergency descent target = 10,000 ft. All four numbers commonly asked.
🪤
VIRGA
VIRGA = rain evaporating before reaching the ground = dry microburst. No rain visible at surface but severe wind shear below.
🪤
Wake turbulence vortex rotation
Viewed from behind: Left vortex = Clockwise. Right vortex = Counter-clockwise. In light crosswind, upwind vortex remains on runway.

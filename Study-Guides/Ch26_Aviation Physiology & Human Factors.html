5 Gas Laws — What Each Explains (DGCA Direct MCQs)
Boyle's
P↓ → Volume↑
Climb = gas expands
Descent = gas compresses
Explains: Trapped Gas
Henry's
P↓ → Gas leaves solution
Like soda bottle uncapped
N₂ bubbles form in body
Explains: Decompression Sickness
Dalton's
Total = Sum of partial pressures
O₂% stays 21% at altitude
But partial pressure drops!
Explains: Hypoxia
Graham's
Diffusion: high → low concentration
O₂: Alveoli → Blood → Tissues
CO₂: Tissues → Blood → Air
Explains: Gas Exchange
Charles'
P ∝ Temperature (constant volume)
Temp↑ → Pressure↑
O₂ cylinders, rapid decompression
Explains: Temp-Pressure Relationship

⚠️ DGCA Exam Traps
🪤
O₂ percentage stays 21% at altitude
At altitude, O₂ is still 21% of the air. But the PARTIAL PRESSURE drops. This is Dalton's Law — lower total pressure means less O₂ per breath. The percentage never changes.
🪤
Breathing is controlled by CO₂, NOT O₂
The primary drive to breathe is CO₂ level in the blood, not oxygen level. That's why hyperventilation (blowing off CO₂) is dangerous — it removes the breathing stimulus.
🪤
Somatogravic: Acceleration = nose-up feel (not nose-down)
Rapid acceleration stimulates otolith organs creating a sensation of pitching up. Pilot may PUSH NOSE DOWN dangerously. Trust instruments!
🪤
Black Hole = LOW approach (not high)
Black hole illusion makes pilot feel TOO HIGH, so they descend MORE than needed. Result = dangerously LOW approach. Classic exam confusion.
🪤
Grand Mal = LOC · Petit Mal = NO LOC
Grand Mal = major seizure WITH loss of consciousness. Petit Mal = minor seizure WITHOUT loss of consciousness. Both disqualify from flying.
🪤
Otolith = Linear · Semicircular = Angular
Otolith organs detect LINEAR acceleration (straight line). Semicircular canals detect ANGULAR (rotational) acceleration. Frequently confused in MCQs.
